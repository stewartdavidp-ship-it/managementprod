Session #47 — Surface Expansion

A simple research review turned into a
system architecture. Here's how.

The developer sat down to review AI platform research. Thirty minutes later, they'd designed a surface intelligence system with task routing, user profiling, and crowd-sourced recommendations. Every insight was captured. Nothing was lost.

Shape
The question behind the question

Research showed which AI platforms could connect via MCP. But the developer pushed past compatibility and asked the question nobody had considered.

"They may support MCP, but how do they support skills? Would we need to rewrite skills per engine?"
Challenge
One observation, two dimensions

The proposed solution used separate parameters for surface and engine. The developer saw that the existing naming convention already encoded both — and collapsed two params into one.

"Today we do claude-code, claude-chat. Why not just do cursor-claude?"
Capture
The architecture emerges

That naming decision triggered a bigger need: if tools adapt per surface, where does the configuration live? The answer — a surface registry — became the architectural centerpiece.

"Create a surface object that tracks what a surface can and cannot do. Let the tools read it."
Refine
Three layers of intelligence

Each captured decision enabled the next. The registry enabled task routing. Task routing enabled user profiling. User profiling enabled crowd-sourced recommendations across platforms.

"Add user subscriptions and actual usage data. Over time this becomes crowd-sourced."

The session ended. The thinking didn't.

Every decision, constraint, and open question was captured as a first-class object. The next session — on any surface, with any AI — picks up exactly where this one left off. No re-explaining. No lost context. No relitigating.
